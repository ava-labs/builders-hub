## Communication & Storytelling

### The Curse of Knowledge

There's a well-documented cognitive bias called the "curse of knowledge." Once you deeply understand something, your brain can no longer easily simulate what it was like to not know it. The concept becomes obvious to you, so you start skipping the steps that made it obvious.

This shows up constantly in Web3 communities. Someone who has been in the Avalanche ecosystem for two years genuinely cannot remember a time when they didn't understand what a subnet was, or why finality speed matters. So when they explain it to a newcomer, they start three levels up the abstraction ladder from where the newcomer actually is.

The newcomer isn't missing something. The expert just started the explanation at the wrong level.

Recognizing that you have this bias is the first step. The second step is building a habit of checking where your audience actually is before you start talking.

### Reading Your Audience

Before explaining anything, take a moment to figure out what the person already knows. A few simple questions help:

- "Have you used any blockchain before, or is this your first time hearing about it?"
- "Are you coming from a technical background, or more from the community/business side?"
- "What brings you here today?"

These aren't gatekeeping questions. They're calibration tools. The answers tell you where to start, what vocabulary is safe to use, and what analogies will actually connect.

The three most common audience types you'll encounter as a Team1 member:

| Audience | Background | What they need |
|---|---|---|
| Developers | Programming, maybe other blockchains | Technical precision, accurate comparisons, code examples |
| Students / Newcomers | Little to no blockchain context | Analogies, "why does this exist", gradual introduction of terms |
| Media / Investors | Business background, skeptical | Proof of real-world utility, track record, plain language |

You don't need three completely different presentations. You need one clear core message and the ability to translate it into different registers.

### The Problem-Solution Framework

The single most useful storytelling structure for explaining blockchain technology is the problem-solution narrative:

1. **Name a problem the audience already feels**, something they've experienced or can easily imagine
2. **Show why existing approaches fail to fully solve it**, without being dismissive of alternatives
3. **Introduce the technology as a better answer**, letting the solution feel earned, not just claimed

This works because it creates stakes before you introduce the concept. If someone doesn't understand why decentralization matters, explaining how Avalanche achieves it is useless. But if they first understand that traditional financial systems can freeze accounts arbitrarily, or that centralized infrastructure has single points of failure, now the concept of decentralization has a job to do.

For example: instead of opening with "Avalanche is a high-throughput, low-latency Layer 1 blockchain with a novel consensus mechanism," try:

> "Imagine you're a developer building a payments app. You need transactions to settle in under a second, and you need fees low enough that a $1 payment doesn't cost more in gas than it's worth. Most blockchains give you one or the other. Avalanche gives you both. And it's built so that anyone can deploy their own custom blockchain on top of it."

Same information, very different entry point.

### Building Analogies That Actually Work

A good analogy does one job: it maps an unfamiliar concept onto something the audience already understands. It doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be functional.

Some analogies that have worked well for explaining Avalanche concepts:

- **The Avalanche network vs. C-Chain**: "Think of Avalanche like a shoe factory. The C-Chain is one type of shoe the factory produces, the flagship model most builders work with. The factory provides all the underlying infrastructure. The C-Chain is just one product running on top of it."
- **Consensus and finality**: "When you make a bank transfer, you often wait days for 'settlement' because banks check and recheck. Avalanche's consensus reaches agreement in about a second, and once it does, it doesn't reverse. Think of it like a handshake that immediately becomes a binding contract."
- **Custom L1s**: "Extending the shoe factory idea: a Custom L1 is like a separate factory that licenses the same manufacturing technology. You hire your own workers, set your own production rules, and make whatever type of shoe you want. It runs independently from the original factory, but is built on the same core process."

A few rules for building your own:

- Pick something from everyday life, not from another technical domain. "Like a microservice" means nothing to someone who doesn't work in software
- Acknowledge when the analogy breaks down: "This comparison works up to a point, then it diverges because..."
- Test it on someone who doesn't know the concept. If they come away more confused, the analogy needs work

### Adapting Without Dumbing Down

There's a meaningful difference between adapting your explanation for an audience and talking down to them.

Adapting means: meeting people where they are, using language that connects, building on what they know.

Dumbing down means: removing nuance, being condescending, treating the audience as incapable of engaging with substance.

A skilled communicator can explain a complex mechanism clearly *and* accurately. The goal is not simplicity for its own sake. It's precision at the right level of abstraction for this particular person, at this particular moment.

When in doubt: ask. "Is this making sense so far?" and "Would you like me to go deeper on that part?" are not signs of uncertainty. They're signs of a communicator who cares whether their message actually landed.

### Key Takeaways

- The curse of knowledge is real: expertise actively makes it harder to explain things to beginners, because you can no longer access the state of not knowing
- Calibrate your language to your audience by asking simple questions before you start explaining
- Use the problem-solution framework to create stakes before introducing the technology
- Build analogies from everyday life, acknowledge where they break, and test them on real people
- Adapting your message is not the same as dumbing it down; accuracy and accessibility can coexist
