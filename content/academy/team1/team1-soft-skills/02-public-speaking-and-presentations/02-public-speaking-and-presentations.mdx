## Public Speaking & Presentations

### Why This Matters for Team1

Most Team1 members will speak in public at some point: at a local meetup, a university event, a hackathon kickoff, or a community workshop. Some of these moments are small: explaining Avalanche to a group of five students around a whiteboard. Some are larger: presenting at a regional conference with an audience you've never met.

The skill transfers across all of them, and the fundamentals are the same whether you're talking to five people or five hundred.

### The One Core Message Rule

Every good talk has one clear answer to the question: "If the audience remembers nothing else, what should they remember?"

This is sometimes called a "spine": everything in the talk hangs on it. Supporting points reinforce it. Examples illustrate it. The opening sets it up. The conclusion restates it.

The most common reason talks fall flat is not lack of content. It's too much content with no spine. The audience can't figure out what they're supposed to take away, so they take away nothing.

Before you start building slides, write one sentence: *"After this talk, the audience will understand/know/be able to [one thing]."*

If you can't write that sentence, you're not ready to build the talk yet.

### The Three-Part Structure

Almost every effective presentation uses some version of this arc:

**1. Opening: establish the stakes**  
In the first 60-90 seconds, give the audience a reason to pay attention. This can be a question, a counterintuitive claim, a short story, or a concrete problem they recognize. Don't open with your credentials or a table of contents. Nobody is invested yet.

**2. Body: deliver the substance**  
This is where the actual content goes. For a 20-minute talk, three to five main points is the right range. Each point should directly support the core message. Use examples, data, or demonstrations to make abstract ideas concrete.

**3. Close: make it land**  
Restate the core message, connect back to the opening if possible, and give the audience one clear thing to do or think about next. A strong close is not "That's everything I have, thank you." It's a deliberate signal that the talk is complete.

### Managing Nerves

Being nervous before speaking is normal and nearly universal, including for experienced speakers. What changes with practice is not the absence of nerves but the relationship you have with them.

The physiological state of nervousness (elevated heart rate, heightened awareness, slightly faster breathing) is almost identical to the physiological state of excitement. Research by psychologist Alison Wood Brooks found that people who told themselves "I'm excited" before a high-pressure task outperformed those who tried to calm down, because they worked with the arousal rather than against it.

Practically, this means:

- Before you go on, don't try to suppress the feeling. Say to yourself: "I'm excited about this"
- Remember that the audience is on your side; they came to learn from you, not to watch you fail
- The first 30 seconds are the hardest. Once you're talking, the state settles
- Preparation is the real antidote to nerves. Knowing your material well enough to recover from a stumble changes how you feel about stumbling

### Workshops vs. Keynotes

These are different formats with different goals, and confusing them is a common mistake.

**A keynote or talk** is a one-to-many format. The speaker carries the experience. The audience's job is to receive and process. Success is measured by whether the audience understood and retained the message.

**A workshop** is a participatory format. The facilitator's job is to design and guide activities, not to lecture continuously. The participants do most of the thinking and talking. Success is measured by whether participants practiced a skill, made something, or changed their perspective through doing.

When you're running a Team1 workshop (say, a hands-on intro to deploying on Avalanche, or a session on how to write a bounty proposal), you should be talking less than 40% of the time. The rest should be structured activities, discussion, or work time.

A common mistake: showing up to a "workshop" with 45 slides and spending 90 minutes presenting them. That's a talk, and it's likely not what participants came for.

### Slide Design That Doesn't Fight You

Slides should support your talk, not duplicate it. If your slides contain everything you're going to say, the audience can read them and stop listening to you.

A few principles that reliably help:

- **One idea per slide.** If you find yourself using bullet points to cram multiple things onto one slide, that's a sign you need more slides or fewer ideas.
- **Visuals over text.** A diagram, a photo, or a short code snippet carries information faster and with less cognitive load than a paragraph.
- **Empty space is not wasted space.** Slides that feel "light" are easier to process than dense ones.
- **Read your slides aloud.** If reading the slide and listening to you at the same time feels impossible, the slide has too much text.

For technical presentations, this applies especially to code examples. Show only the relevant lines. Use syntax highlighting. Give the audience a few seconds to read before you explain it.

### Handling Q&A Honestly

Q&A is where credibility is built or lost, and the most credibility-building move you can make is simple: say "I don't know" when you don't know.

Audiences can tell the difference between a fabricated answer and an honest one. A fabricated answer might go undetected in the moment, but if it's wrong and someone checks later, the damage is far worse than admitting uncertainty would have been.

Some practical approaches:

- **Buy a second**: "That's a good question. Let me make sure I understand it before I answer." This is not stalling. It's respect for accuracy.
- **Bridge to what you do know**: "I'm not certain about the specific number, but what I can tell you is how the mechanism works..."
- **Acknowledge and follow up**: "I don't have a confident answer to that right now, but I'll find out and post in Discord after." Then actually do it. The follow-up creates a lasting impression.
- **Redirect to the room**: "I'm not sure. Does anyone else here have direct experience with that?" This is fine for discussions; avoid it for factual questions where it looks like deflection.

### Key Takeaways

- Every talk needs one clear core message; everything else should support it
- Use the three-part structure: open with stakes, deliver substance, close with a landing
- Reframe nervousness as excitement; work with the physiological state rather than against it
- Workshops are participatory formats; if you're talking more than 40% of the time, it's not a workshop
- Slides should support you, not replace you: one idea per slide, visuals over text
- Saying "I don't know" in Q&A builds more credibility than a guessed answer that might be wrong
